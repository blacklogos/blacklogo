# Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Template
## Marketing Performance Review for Leadership

---

## Purpose of QBR

The Quarterly Business Review provides leadership with:
- Clear picture of marketing performance vs. goals
- Analysis of what's working and what's not
- Data-driven recommendations for next quarter
- Budget performance and ROI analysis
- Alignment on priorities and resource allocation

**Audience:** CEO, CFO, VP Sales, Leadership Team
**Duration:** 30-45 minutes
**Frequency:** End of each quarter (within 2 weeks of quarter close)

## QBR Preparation Checklist

**2 Weeks Before:**
- [ ] Pull data from all marketing platforms
- [ ] Compile sales data and attribution
- [ ] Survey sales team for feedback
- [ ] Analyze campaign performance
- [ ] Update competitive intelligence

**1 Week Before:**
- [ ] Create slides with data and analysis
- [ ] Identify key insights and recommendations
- [ ] Review with marketing team
- [ ] Get sales feedback on draft
- [ ] Refine messaging and asks

**2 Days Before:**
- [ ] Finalize presentation
- [ ] Prepare backup slides for deep-dive questions
- [ ] Rehearse presentation
- [ ] Send read-ahead to attendees
- [ ] Prepare Q&A responses

**Day Of:**
- [ ] Test technology and screen sharing
- [ ] Print handouts (if in-person)
- [ ] Have data sources ready for questions
- [ ] Bring positive energy and confidence

**After QBR:**
- [ ] Distribute slides and notes
- [ ] Document decisions and action items
- [ ] Follow up on asks and commitments
- [ ] Adjust plans based on feedback
- [ ] Schedule check-ins for major initiatives

## Tips for Effective QBRs

**Do:**
- Lead with business impact (revenue, pipeline)
- Use visuals and charts for data
- Be honest about what's not working
- Come with clear recommendations
- Show how you're learning and adapting
- Connect marketing to company goals
- Celebrate wins with the team

**Don't:**
- Dump data without insights
- Make excuses for misses
- Ask for more budget without ROI justification
- Present problems without solutions
- Go over time - respect busy executives
- Use marketing jargon
- Surprise leadership with bad news
